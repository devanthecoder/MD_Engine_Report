\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This report presents the design, implementation, and validation of a Molecular
Dynamics (MD) engine was built entirely from scratch to simulate Krypton, a noble
gas well described by the Lennard-Jones pair potential. The engine was assembled
in six self-contained modules covering the interaction model, periodic boundary conditions, numerical time integration via the Velocity Verlet algorithm,
performance optimisation through Verlet neighbour lists and NumPy vectorisation,
system initialisation with Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity sampling, and structural
analysis via the Radial Distribution Function $g(r)$. Each module was
independently tested before integration into a single simulation pipeline. The
engine was validated against three stability criteria: relative energy
fluctuations below $10^{-4}$, zero net linear momentum to machine precision, and
no systematic energy drift during the production phase. Simulations of Krypton
at $\rho^* = 0.8442$ across six temperatures from $T = 5$\,K to
$T = 162.5$\,K revealed a clear solid-to-liquid transition through the
progressive collapse of sharp crystalline peaks in $g(r)$ into a broad liquid
signature. The simulated melting point, bracketed at $T^*_\text{melt} \approx
0.70$ ($T \approx 114$\, K), lies within 2\% of the experimental value of $115.79$\, K.
\end{abstract}

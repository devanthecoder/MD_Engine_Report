\section{Module 4: Performance and Optimisation}

\subsection{What Are We Doing?}

By Module 3, we had a working MD engine. The core operation (computing the force on every atom due to every other atom) has a computational cost that grows catastrophically with the number of particles. Module 4 confronts this bottleneck head-on with two optimisations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Verlet Neighbour Lists}: a bookkeeping trick that eliminates the vast majority of pointless distance calculations.
    \item \textbf{NumPy Vectorisation}: rewriting the force loop as bulk array operations, preventing overhead by orders of magnitude.
\end{enumerate}

Together, these changes transform the code from a toy that handles $N \sim 100$ atoms to an engine capable of simulating the full $N = 256$ system described in our project brief.\\[0.5em]
    Additionally, we make use of a \textbf{Shifted-Force Potential} instead of the Shifted Potential implemented in Module 1. This is so that Force and Potential are both set to zero at the cutoff, hence preventing artificial energy changes.

\subsection{Why Are We Doing It?}

\subsubsection{The \texorpdfstring{$O(N^2)$}{O(N²)} Bottleneck: An Intuitive Picture}

Imagine a busy restaurant kitchen with $N$ chefs, and a head chef who must check whether any two chefs are about to collide and spill something. If he does this by checking every possible pair, he makes $N(N-1)/2$ checks per sweep. Double the kitchen staff, and his workload \emph{quadruples}. This is the essence of $O(N^2)$ scaling.

The naive force loop does exactly this: for $N$ atoms, it checks every possible pair $(i, j)$, performing $N(N-1)/2$ distance calculations per time step. The table below shows how rapidly this becomes unmanageable:

        256  ($4^3$ unit cells) & 32{,}640  & 5.7$\times$ \\

The cruel irony is that the overwhelming majority of these checks are \emph{wasted}. Our LJ potential is truncated at $r_c^* = 2.5$: any two atoms further apart than this cutoff contribute \emph{exactly zero} force to one another. Yet the naive loop dutifully computes the separation of every pair anyway, at every time step, only to discard the result. For $N = 500$ at $\rho^* = 0.8$, only ${\sim}9{,}400$ out of $124{,}750$ pairs actually lie within the cutoff. \textbf{Over 92\% of all distance calculations produce nothing useful.}

A smarter head chef does not watch every possible pair of chefs simultaneously. Instead, he glances around once, notes which chefs are working near each other, and watches only those pairs for the next little while. He rebuilds his mental list only when someone moves far enough that it might have gone stale, and he pads it with a small buffer: anyone within arm's reach of the danger zone gets noted down, even if not yet close enough to collide. That buffer buys him several seconds before the list needs updating.

% Last paragraph of 4.2.1
This is precisely the idea behind the \textbf{Verlet Neighbour List}. Rather than 
recomputing all $N(N-1)/2$ pairs at every step, we build a list of nearby atom pairs 
once and reuse it for several time steps. By pre-filtering to only those pairs within 
a slightly enlarged radius $r_s > r_c$ (the skin radius), we drastically reduce the 
number of pairs we ever need to consider, with the full $O(N^2)$ scan triggered only 
occasionally when atoms have drifted far enough to potentially invalidate the list. 
The question is: how do we choose $r_s$, and how do we know when the list has gone stale?

\subsubsection{Why the Skin Radius Allows Infrequent Rebuilds}

The answer lies in a careful choice of the buffer between $r_s$ and $r_c$. We maintain 
a pre-computed list of all pairs within $r_s$, computing forces only for those that also 
fall within $r_c$, and ignore everything beyond $r_s$ entirely.

The key insight is the \textbf{skin buffer}: we build the list with a radius $r_s = r_c + \delta_\text{skin}$, slightly larger than the interaction cutoff. Any atom currently outside $r_s$ would need to travel at least $\delta_\text{skin}$ before it could possibly enter the interaction zone. Since atoms move slowly relative to $\delta_\text{skin}$ over a few time steps, the list remains valid (without a single rebuild) for many steps at a time.

We track this safety condition explicitly. After each Velocity Verlet step, we measure the maximum displacement $\Delta r_\text{max}$ of any atom from its position at the last rebuild and update the list when the below condition satisfies:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta r_\text{max} > \frac{r_s - r_c}{2} = \frac{\delta_\text{skin}}{2}
    \label{eq:rebuild_criterion}
\end{equation}

The factor of 2 appears because \emph{two} atoms can simultaneously drift by $\delta_\text{skin}/2$ toward/away from each other, collectively covering the entire buffer in a single step. Dividing by 2 is the conservative safe-guard.

\subsubsection{Why the Shifted-Force Potential Improves Energy Conservation}

Module 1 introduced the \emph{shifted potential}: subtract $\phi^*(r_c^*)$ from the LJ formula so that the energy goes smoothly to zero at the cutoff. This fixed the energy discontinuity. However, subtracting a constant from the potential has no effect on its derivative, and the derivative is the force. So the force magnitude still jumps abruptly from $F^*(r_c^-)$ down to zero the moment atoms cross $r_c^*$.

Every time a pair crosses the cutoff, the system gains or loses a small artificial energy kick. Over thousands of time steps these micro-kicks accumulate into a systematic energy drift.

So, we want both the potential \emph{and} the force to vanish continuously at $r_c^*$. Define the \textbf{shifted force}:
\begin{equation}
    F_\text{SF}^*(r^*) \;\equiv\; F^*(r^*) - \frac{F^*(r_c^*)}{r_c^*}\,r^*
    \label{eq:sf_force_def}
\end{equation}
At $r^* = r_c^*$: $F_\text{SF}^*(r^*) \;\equiv\; F^*(r^*) - \dfrac{F^*(r_c^*)}{r_c^*}\cdot r^*_c = F^*(r^*) -F^*(r^*)=0$, confirming the \\[0.5em] validity of our Shifted-Force Potential. 

Note that the correction is \emph{not} simply $F^*(r_c^*)$ (a constant), but $\bigl[F^*(r_c^*)/r_c^*\bigr]\cdot r^*$ (a linear function of $r^*$) because the force vector in 3D is $\mathbf{F} = (F/r)\,\mathbf{r}$, and we must subtract a correction that preserves this structure. We label the scalar quantity $F^*(r_c^*)/r_c^*$ as the constant $C$ in our implementation.

The corresponding \textbf{Shifted-Force potential} is found by integrating:
\begin{equation}
    \phi_\text{SF}^*(r^*) \;=\; -\int_{r_c^*}^{r^*} F_\text{SF}^*(r)\,dr
    \label{eq:sf_pot_integral}
\end{equation}
Splitting the integral:
\begin{align}
    \phi_\text{SF}^*(r^*) &= -\int_{r_c^*}^{r^*} F^*(r)\,dr \;+\; \frac{F^*(r_c^*)}{r_c^*}\int_{r_c^*}^{r^*} r\,dr \notag \\
    &= \bigl[\phi^*(r^*) - \phi^*(r_c^*)\bigr] \;+\; \frac{F^*(r_c^*)}{r_c^*}\cdot\frac{(r^*)^2 - (r_c^*)^2}{2}
    \label{eq:sf_pot_final}
\end{align}
where we used $F^* = -\dfrac{d\phi^*}{dr^*}$, so $-\int F^*\,dr = \phi^*(r^*) - \phi^*(r_c^*)$.

Equation~\eqref{eq:sf_pot_final} is directly encoded in our Module 4 implementation as:
\[
    \texttt{potE} = \underbrace{\phi^* - \phi_c^*}_{\text{shifted pot.}} \;+\; \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\,C\,((r^*)^2 - (r_c^*)^2)}_{\text{quadratic correction}}
\]
where \texttt{force\_by\_rc} plays the role of $C = F^*(r_c^*)/r_c^*$, and all quantities are in terms of $r^2$ to avoid unnecessary square roots.

\paragraph{What this achieves in practice.}
Figure~\ref{fig:sf_potential} shows the two curves side by side: the shifted potential (from Module 1) has a zero in the energy but a jump in the force; the shifted-force potential has zeros in \emph{both}. The linear correction introduced in Eq.~\eqref{eq:sf_pot_final} is small (the two potentials are nearly indistinguishable at distances well inside the cutoff) but eliminates the force discontinuity entirely.

\subsubsection{Why Vectorisation Helps So Much}

Python's \texttt{for} loops are slow because the interpreter re-evaluates every iteration (type-checking, memory allocation) thousands of times per loop. NumPy bypasses all of this by operating on contiguous memory blocks in compiled C++ code. A loop over $N^2$ pairs that takes seconds in pure Python completes in milliseconds as a vectorised array subtraction and sum.

The speedup comes not from a better algorithm but from delegating the numerical heavy-lifting to the hardware-optimised NumPy backend. The pair-selection logic (``which atoms are within $r_s$?'') becomes a single boolean mask on a 2D distance matrix, and the force accumulation becomes an indexed scatter via \texttt{np.add.at}.

\subsection{How Is It Implemented?}

\subsubsection{Building the Neighbour List}

At the start of the simulation and whenever Eq.~\eqref{eq:rebuild_criterion} is triggered, we compute all pairwise separations using the Minimum Image Convention and store every pair $(i, j)$ with $i < j$ whose squared separation is less than $r_s^{*2}$. Because we work with $r^2$ throughout, we never have to take a square root.

Between rebuilds, the displacement of each atom from its position at the last rebuild is tracked. The rebuild is triggered only when any single atom's displacement exceeds $\delta_\text{skin}/2$, ensuring no newly relevant neighbour can have been missed.

\subsubsection{Evaluating Forces with the Neighbour List}

Once the list is built, force evaluation is straightforward: we extract only the indexed positions of listed pairs, compute their separations and squared distances in one vectorised step, apply the cutoff mask (so pairs in the skin buffer but beyond $r_c$ contribute nothing), and compute the LJ shifted-force for all surviving pairs simultaneously. Newton's third law is enforced by scattering $+\mathbf{f}_{ij}$ onto atom $j$ and $-\mathbf{f}_{ij}$ onto atom $i$ in a single indexed-add operation.

\subsubsection{The Displaced Velocity Verlet Step}

The integration step in Module 4 is identical to Module 3, but the force subroutine now calls \texttt{calculate\_forces\_optimised} (with the neighbour list) instead of the all-pairs routine. The displacement tracking is woven into the main loop: after each step, the squared displacement of each atom is checked against the rebuild threshold, and a list update is triggered only when necessary.

\subsubsection{Neighbour List Rebuild Frequency}

For a production run with $N = 32$ atoms, $T^* \approx 1.5$, $\delta t^* = 0.005$, and skin thickness $\delta_\text{skin} = 0.7\,\sigma$, the list was rebuilt \textbf{69 times over 500 steps}: once every $\approx 7$ steps on average. This means the optimised routine avoids a full $O(N^2)$ scan for approximately \textbf{86\% of all time steps}, paying only the cost of a displacement-check array operation per step.

\section{Physical Property Analysis: Krypton}

\subsection{From Reduced Units to Real Krypton}

Everything so far has lived in the dimensionless world of reduced units: distances in multiples of $\sigma$, temperatures in multiples of $\eps/k_B$. Coming back to with reality, we need to attach physical meaning to these numbers. Krypton is an ideal test species: it is a noble gas well described by the Lennard-Jones model, with accurately measured parameters tabulated in literature.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Lennard-Jones parameters for Krypton (from LeSar, Table 5.1). These two numbers, together with the atomic mass $m_\text{Kr} = 83.80$\,u, define the complete simulation.}
    \label{tab:kr_params}
    \begin{tabular}{llcc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Symbol} & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Units} \\
        \midrule
        LJ well depth       & $\eps$          & $0.0140$   & eV \\
        Well depth (thermal)& $\eps/k_B$      & $162.46$   & K \\
        LJ diameter         & $\sigma$        & $3.65$     & \AA \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The conversion from any reduced quantity to its SI/cgs counterpart follows from the definitions in Eq.~\eqref{eq:reduced_units}. For the two quantities needed here:
\begin{align}
    r\,[\text{\AA}] &= r^* \times \sigma_{\text{Kr}} = r^* \times 3.65\,\text{\AA}
    \label{eq:r_conv}\\
    T\,[\text{K}]   &= T^* \times \frac{\eps_{\text{Kr}}}{k_B} = T^* \times 162.46\,\text{K}
    \label{eq:T_conv}
\end{align}

We ran the MD engine at two representative state points chosen to probe opposite ends of the phase diagram:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $T^* = 0.2$ ($T \approx 32.5$\,K), which is well below the expected melting point; the system should be a crystalline FCC solid.
    \item $T^* = 1.0$ ($T \approx 162.5$\,K), which is near and above the expected liquid regime for Krypton at this density; the system should be a disordered liquid.
\end{itemize}
Both runs used $N = 500$ atoms at $\rho^* = 0.8442$ (box length $L^* = 8.398$, i.e.\ $L = 30.65$\,\AA), with $g(r)$ averaged over 100 production frames after full equilibration.

\subsection{State Identification and Physical Interpretation}

\subsubsection{Solid Phase ($T^* = 0.2$, $T \approx 32.5$\,K)}
At $T^* = 0.2$, Krypton is solidly in its crystalline FCC phase. The $g(r)$ computed from the simulation reflects this directly: it shows distinct, sharply resolved peaks before reaching the box cutoff at $r^* = L^*/2 \approx 3.36$, with near-zero valleys between them.
The peaks arise because atoms are vibrating about fixed lattice sites. Each crystallographic coordination shell, defined as the set of neighbours at a specific geometry-dictated distance, contributes one delta-function-like spike that is only slightly broadened by thermal motion at this low temperature. Crucially, these peaks do not die away with increasing $r$. This is the hallmark of \emph{long-range order}: knowing where one atom is tells you, with high probability, exactly where atoms are 15\,\AA\ away. The valleys between peaks approach zero, meaning that finding an atom at those interstitial distances is nearly forbidden by the lattice geometry.

To check quantitatively whether the peak positions match genuine FCC geometry, we compare the measured peak positions to the theoretical FCC coordination shell distances. The simulation uses $N = 256$ atoms ($4 \times 4^3$ unit cells) in a cubic box of side $L^* = 6.7184$ at density $\rho^* = 0.8442$. The FCC lattice cell parameter in reduced units is:
\begin{equation}
    a^* = \left(\frac{4}{\rho^*}\right)^{1/3} = \left(\frac{4}{0.8442}\right)^{1/3} \approx 1.6796
    \label{eq:fcc_lat}
\end{equation}
The nearest-neighbour distance is the face-diagonal divided by two ($r^*_1 = a^*/\sqrt{2} \approx 1.1877$), or in ångströms $r_1 = 1.1877 \times 3.65 \approx 4.335$\,\AA. Table~\ref{tab:fcc_peaks} lists the first five FCC nearest neighbour distances and compares them to the peak positions extracted algorithmically from the simulated $g(r)$ at $T^* = 0.2$, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:kr_gr_solid}.

The agreement is excellent to within $\sim 0.11$\,\AA\ across all five shells. The small systematic shift of simulation peaks to slightly shorter distances relative to the ideal values reflects that at $T^* = 0.2$, finite thermal pressure causes a slight contraction of the effective lattice spacing compared to the zero-temperature FCC ideal. The ratios of successive peak positions in the simulation ($1.000 : 1.435 : 1.753 : 2.043 : 2.275$) are in close agreement with the theoretical FCC ratios ($1 : \sqrt{2} : \sqrt{3} : 2 : \sqrt{5}$, i.e.\ $1.000 : 1.414 : 1.732 : 2.000 : 2.236$), confirming the crystalline symmetry.

The first peak height is $g(r^*_1) \approx 4.578$. The large magnitude, almost 4.6 times the bulk density, reflects that the 12 nearest neighbours in the FCC lattice are all clustered within a narrow radial band, a consequence of the crystal's perfect translational symmetry. Note that the even-numbered shells (2nd and 4th) exhibit markedly lower peak heights of $g \approx 0.894$ and $0.931$ respectively. This is because the $g(r)$ normalisation divides by the shell volume, and these shells collect fewer neighbours than the adjacent odd shells, causing the apparent height to be suppressed relative to the bulk average.

\subsubsection{Running Coordination Number ($T^* = 0.2$)}

A complementary and more direct way to quantify the shell structure of the solid is through the \emph{running coordination number} $M(r^*)$, defined as the mean number of atoms found within a sphere of reduced radius $r^*$ centred on a given atom:
\begin{equation}
    M(r^*) = 4\pi\rho^* \int_{0}^{r^*} r'^{2}\, g(r')\, \mathrm{d}r'.
    \label{eq:running_coord}
\end{equation}
In the simulation, this integral is evaluated numerically. At each radial bin, the integrand $4\pi\rho^* r^2 g(r)$ is formed from the production-averaged $g(r)$, and \texttt{numpy.trapezoid} accumulates the running sum from $r^* = 0$ outwards, yielding $M(r^*)$ as a continuous curve over the 100 snapshots collected during the production run. The resulting curve is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:kr_running_coord}.

Rather than rising smoothly as it would in a liquid, $M(r^*)$ climbs in a staircase of well-defined plateaux. The key distinction in interpreting this curve is between two related but separate quantities:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Shell coordination number $\Delta M_k$}: the number of atoms belonging exclusively to shell $k$, obtained as the \emph{difference} $M(r^*_{\min,k}) - M(r^*_{\min,k-1})$, where $r^*_{\min,k}$ is the position of the $k$-th minimum of $g(r)$ (i.e.\ the upper boundary of shell $k$) and $r^*_{\min,0} \equiv 0$. This is the height of the $k$-th step in the staircase.
    \item \textbf{Cumulative coordination number $M_k$}: the total number of atoms within radius $r^*_{\min,k}$, i.e.\ summed over all shells up to and including shell $k$: $M_k = \sum_{j=1}^{k} \Delta M_j$. This is the plateau \emph{value} reached after the $k$-th step.
\end{itemize}
The minima of $g(r)$ that define the shell boundaries are located algorithmically: for each shell, we scan forward from the $k$-th peak index and identify the first index at which $g(r)$ satisfies the discrete local-minimum condition $g_i \leq g_{i-1}$ and $g_i < g_{i+1}$ (or the symmetric case). The table below reports both $\Delta M_k$ and $M_k$ for the first five shells.

The small deviations of $\Delta M_k$ from the ideal integer values in the first four shells are primarily due to thermal motion: at $T^* = 0.2$, atoms vibrate about their lattice sites, slightly smearing each coordination shell and allowing a small number of atoms to sit near the boundary between shells rather than cleanly inside one. The larger discrepancy in the fifth shell ($\Delta M_5 \approx 27.82$ versus the ideal 24) occurs due to the reduced spacing between shells 5 and 6 ($\approx 0.15a^*$), due to which the thermal motion of the atoms (their vibrational amplitude) becomes comparable to this inter-shell spacing. This leads to a not insignificant contribution of leaked neighbours from shell 6.

The staircase shape of $M(r^*)$ is itself the clearest signature of a crystal: in a liquid or gas, atoms are not confined to discrete shells and the curve would rise smoothly with no visible steps.

\subsubsection{Liquid Phase ($T^* = 1.0$, $T \approx 162.5$\,K)}

At $T^* = 1.0$, Krypton is in the liquid state. The $g(r)$ is unambiguously liquid-like: a single prominent but \emph{broad} first peak, followed by weak secondary oscillations that quickly damp to 1.0, with no structure surviving beyond $\sim 10$\,\AA.

The first peak is centred at $r \approx 3.99$\,\AA\ with $g \approx 2.71$. Its position is actually slightly inside the FCC ideal of $4.335$\,\AA. In a liquid, atoms are free to rearrange, and the effective nearest-neighbour distance shifts to the potential minimum near $r^*_\text{min} = 2^{1/6} \approx 1.122$, which corresponds to $r \approx 4.10$\,\AA, close to what we observe.

Beyond the first peak, the solid showed five sharp rings while the liquid shows just one broad bump (around $7.5$\,\AA) before going flat. This means the liquid only has short-range order: atoms keep a well-defined nearest-neighbour cage since they cannot overlap, but beyond that, all positional regularity is lost within a shell or two. Unlike a crystal, a fluid atom has no way of knowing where atoms are more than a couple of diameters away.

The reduced peak height ($g \approx 2.71$ vs.\ $4.44$ in the solid) reflects the much broader angular spread of the neighbours: instead of being locked into 12 precise FCC positions, the liquid first shell spans a diffuse cloud over all directions.

\subsection{Normalization Check: \texorpdfstring{$g(r) \to 1$}{g(r) -> 1}}

A correct $g(r)$ calculation must asymptote to 1.0 at large $r$. This is not merely a convention; it is a direct test of whether the normalization by shell volume and bulk density was implemented without error. Table~\ref{tab:gr_norm} records the mean of the last five histogram bins, near $r \approx L/2 = 4.199\,\sigma = 15.33$\,\AA, for both runs.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Normalization check: mean $g(r)$ over last five bins near $r = L/2$.}
    \label{tab:gr_norm}
    \begin{tabular}{lccc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{State}   & \textbf{$T^*$} & \textbf{Mean $g(r)$ at large $r$} & \textbf{Deviation from 1.0} \\
        \midrule
        Solid (Kr)       & $0.2$          & $0.963$                            & $-3.7\%$ \\
        Liquid (Kr)      & $1.0$          & $1.017$                            & $+1.7\%$ \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The liquid result ($1.017$) is within $1.7\%$ of the theoretical asymptote, which is a clear pass consistent with the Module 6 test at $T^* = 1.0$ which gave $0.9951$. The normalization formula is correct.

The solid result ($0.963$) needs a careful reading. A $-3.7\%$ deviation might look like a normalisation error, but it is actually a physical consequence of the crystal's long-range order. Unlike a liquid, $g(r)$ in a crystal does not smoothly settle to $1.0$. It keeps oscillating out to large $r$ because the atoms retain positional correlations across the whole box. At $T^* = 0.2$ these oscillations have not died out by $r = L/2$, so the last few bins land on a descending part of a peak rather than the flat asymptote. A larger simulation box would fix this by giving the oscillations more room to decay before the $L/2$ cutoff is reached. The physics is correct; the apparent offset is simply a finite-size artificial thing amplified by the solid's long-range order, not a bug.

In summary: for a \emph{liquid}, $g(r) \to 1.0$ serves as a clean normalization test; for a \emph{solid}, residual structure near $L/2$ is expected and physically meaningful. Both state points are consistent with their respective expected behaviours, and the MD engine faithfully reproduces the structural fingerprint of Krypton in both phases.

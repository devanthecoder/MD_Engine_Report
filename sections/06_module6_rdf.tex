\section{Module 6: Radial Distribution Function}

\subsection{What Are We Doing?}

After building and validating the MD engine, we now need to extract meaningful physical information from the atomic trajectories it generates. The question we want to answer is: \textit{how are the atoms arranged in space, and does that arrangement look like a solid, a liquid, or something in between?} Module 6 implements the \textbf{Radial Distribution Function} $g(r)$, the standard structural metric of statistical mechanics, which serves as a quantitative ``fingerprint'' of the material's phase.

\subsection{Why Are We Doing It?}

\subsubsection{Why Is Temperature Not Enough?}

It might seem natural to identify the phase of a system from its temperature alone — after all, we speak of the ``melting point'' as a specific temperature. But in a simulation at fixed density, the same temperature can correspond to different phases depending on the pressure, and the kinetic energy (temperature) carries no information about \emph{geometry}. Two configurations with completely different atomic arrangements — one crystalline, one disordered — can have the same temperature and even the same total energy.

What distinguishes a solid from a liquid is not energy but \emph{spatial order}: whether atoms sit in predictable positions relative to one another. $g(r)$ captures exactly this. It is defined as the probability of finding an atom at distance $r$ from a reference atom, relative to what a completely random (ideal gas) arrangement would predict:
\begin{equation}
    g(r) = \frac{V}{4\pi r^2 N^2}
    \left\langle \sum_i \sum_{j \neq i} \delta(r - r_{ij}) \right\rangle
    \label{eq:gr_def}
\end{equation}
Because $g(r)$ is normalized against the average bulk density $\rho = N/V$, a perfectly uniform system gives $g(r) = 1$ everywhere. Peaks above 1 mark distances where atoms preferentially cluster; valleys below 1 mark excluded regions.

\subsubsection{Solid vs.\ Liquid: The Structural Fingerprint}

The shape of $g(r)$ tells the full story of the phase:

\textbf{Solid:} Atoms are locked near their FCC lattice sites. Each shell of neighbours sits at a precise, geometry-dictated distance — the first shell at $r^* = 1\,\sigma$, the second at $\sqrt{2}\,\sigma$, the third at $\sqrt{3}\,\sigma$, and so on. $g(r)$ shows sharp, narrow peaks at these distances and is nearly zero in between. Crucially, this structure persists to large $r$ — the solid has \emph{long-range order}.

\textbf{Liquid:} Atoms are mobile and constantly rearranging. There is still a well-defined first neighbourhood shell (atoms cannot overlap), producing a broad first peak. But beyond that, the positional correlation decays rapidly. After two or three broad peaks, $g(r)$ settles to 1.0, reflecting that at large distances the liquid looks like a random arrangement. This is the signature of \emph{short-range order only}.

\subsubsection{Why Normalise by Shell Volume and Density?}

Consider building a histogram of how many atoms are found at each distance. Even in a completely featureless ideal gas, this histogram would grow as $r^2$ — simply because there is more \emph{volume} at larger radii. A spherical shell at radius $r$ with thickness $\Delta r$ has volume $4\pi r^2 \Delta r$, so raw counts naturally increase quadratically with $r$. Dividing by the shell volume removes this geometric bias and gives the \emph{local number density} at each radius. Dividing once more by the global bulk density $\rho = N/V$ converts this into a dimensionless ratio. The result is $g(r)$: a function that measures how much denser or sparser the local environment is compared to the system average. By construction, $g(r) \to 1$ at large $r$, where correlations vanish and the local density matches the bulk.

\subsection{How Is It Implemented?}

\subsubsection{Spherical Shell Histogramming}

Figure~\ref{fig:m6_shells} illustrates the core idea. For a given configuration snapshot, every unique pair $(i, j)$ is examined, their separation $r_{ij}$ is computed using the Minimum Image Convention, and the pair is assigned to a histogram bin $k = \lfloor r_{ij} / \Delta r \rfloor$. The bin width is $\Delta r = r_{\max}/n_b$ where $r_{\max} = L/2$ (the MIC limit) and $n_b = 75$ bins in our implementation.

\begin{figure}[H]

    \includegraphics[width=0.62\linewidth]{figures/m6_shells_schematic.pdf}
    \caption{Spherical shell histogram bins for the $g(r)$ calculation. Each coloured annular ring represents one histogram bin of width $\Delta r$ centred at radius $r_k$. The raw count of neighbouring atoms (blue) in each shell must be divided by the shell volume $4\pi r_k^2 \Delta r$ to remove the geometric $r^2$ bias before normalization by the bulk density.}
    \label{fig:m6_shells}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{The Normalization Formula}

After accumulating the histogram $h_k$ (count of pairs at distance $r_k$), the normalized $g(r)$ is:
\begin{equation}
    g(r_k) = \frac{2 \cdot h_k \cdot V}{N^2 \cdot 4\pi r_k^2 \Delta r}
    \label{eq:gr_norm}
\end{equation}
The factor of 2 arises because the code counts each pair $(i, j)$ once (the upper triangle of the distance matrix), while the standard formula's double sum $\sum_{i \neq j}$ counts each pair twice — once as $(i,j)$ and once as $(j,i)$. Multiplying by 2 compensates for this, giving the correct normalization.

\subsubsection{Role of the Minimum Image Convention}

The MIC serves two roles here. First, it ensures every computed distance $r_{ij}$ is the \emph{shortest} possible path between atoms $i$ and $j$ across the periodic tiling, so the structural peaks reflect the true geometry of the material rather than artefacts of the box boundaries. Second, because all MIC distances are bounded by $L/2$, setting $r_{\max} = L/2$ guarantees that no pair is double-counted through opposite periodic images.

\subsubsection{Statistical Averaging Over Frames}

A single snapshot is too noisy to be useful — the histogram bins contain only a handful of counts, and the resulting $g(r)$ would be jagged and unreliable. Moreover, consecutive MD timesteps are highly correlated: the configuration at step $t$ is nearly identical to step $t+1$. The solution is to collect $g(r)$ from multiple snapshots during the production run (every 10 steps in our implementation) and average the results:
\begin{equation}
    \langle g(r_k) \rangle = \frac{1}{N_\text{frames}} \sum_{\text{frames}} g(r_k)
    \label{eq:gr_avg}
\end{equation}
Averaging over 60 frames (from 600 production steps sampled every 10) reduces statistical noise substantially while keeping the frames sufficiently spaced to limit correlation.

\subsubsection{Vectorized Implementation}

The function \texttt{calculate\_rdf} uses fully vectorized NumPy operations. All pairwise displacement vectors are computed simultaneously via broadcasting and the MIC, the upper-triangle indices extract unique pairs without loops, and \texttt{np.bincount} fills the histogram in a single pass. This avoids $O(N^2)$ Python-level iteration entirely.

\subsection{Test Results}

A test simulation was run at $T^* = 1.0$ (liquid-like state), $\rho^* = 0.8442$, $N = 256$ atoms, with 800 equilibration steps and $g(r)$ averaged over 60 production frames.

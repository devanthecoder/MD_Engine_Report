\section{Final Project Challenge: Melting of Krypton}

\subsection{The Question We Are Asking}

So far, we have characterised Krypton at two fixed temperatures: a deep solid at $T^* = 0.2$ and a clear liquid at $T^* = 1.0$. But what actually \emph{happens} in between? Is the solid-to-liquid transition sharp and abrupt, or does structure degrade gradually? And where does it occur?

To answer this, we drove the same Krypton system through a sequence of six temperatures, reading the $g(r)$ fingerprint at each stage. The experiment is the computational equivalent of placing a Krypton crystal on a hot plate and watching it melt, except that instead of observing it by eye, we read the structural order directly from the atomic positions.

\subsection{Protocol}

Rather than running six independent simulations from scratch, we ran a single \textbf{continuous temperature sweep}. The system begins at $T = 5.0$\,K in a near-perfect FCC crystal. The temperature is then stepped upward in six stages. At each stage, the system is \textbf{re-equilibrated} at the new target temperature (2000 steps of Velocity Verlet with periodic velocity rescaling every 10 steps), and then the $g(r)$ is collected over 2000 production steps, sampled every 10 steps for \textbf{200 averaged frames}.

This continuous approach is physically motivated: just as a real crystal does not forget its history when heated, our simulated solid carries its structural memory from one temperature to the next, allowing the phase transition to unfold naturally rather than artificially.

Every intermediate curve shows a different stage of this collapse, and the change is not gradual: the most dramatic shift occurs between $T = 97.48$\,K and $T = 129.97$\,K, indicating a phase transition in that range.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{$T = 5.0$\,K ($T^* = 0.031$): Ultra-Cold Crystal}{T = 5 K: Ultra-Cold Crystal}}

At $T = 5$\,K, the Krypton atoms have barely any thermal energy above zero. The first $g(r)$ peak reaches $g \approx 11.6$, more than eleven times the bulk density, because all twelve nearest FCC neighbours are locked into an almost perfectly narrow radial band. The valleys between peaks drop to essentially zero. The system retains crystal-sharp structure past $r = 10$\,\AA, meaning an atom at the origin can reliably predict where atoms sit nearly three lattice constants away. This is \emph{long-range order}.

The atoms vibrate only slightly around their equilibrium positions. The $g(r)$ is essentially a snapshot of the FCC geometry itself, only slightly smeared by residual classical motion.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{$T = 32.49$\,K ($T^* = 0.200$): Deep Solid}{T = 32.5 K: Deep Solid}}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figures/melting_gr_32k.pdf}
    \caption{g(r) of Kr at T* = 0.2 (32.49 K)}
    \label{fig:placeholder}
\end{figure}
By $T^* = 0.2$, the atoms have gained enough kinetic energy to vibrate noticeably around their lattice positions, but they are nowhere near escaping their potential wells. The first peak drops to $g \approx 5.3$ and is visibly wider than at 5\,K. Later coordination shells are still resolved but their peaks are shorter and broader. The solid is clearly intact, with five or more distinct shells visible, though the atomic positions now form a spread cloud around each ideal lattice site rather than a point.

 The peak positions match FCC geometry to within $\sim 0.15$\,\AA, and the long-range oscillations persist past the box cutoff. The material is a warm but well-ordered crystalline solid.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{$T = 64.98$\,K ($T^* = 0.400$): Warm Solid}{T = 65 K: Warm Solid}}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figures/melting_gr_64k.pdf}
    \caption{g(r) of Kr at T* =0.4(64.98 K)}
    \label{fig:placeholder}
\end{figure}

At $T^* = 0.4$, the peak at the first shell is still sharp and tall ($g \approx 4.1$), but the later shells are beginning to deteriorate. The third and fourth peaks are noticeably broader and shorter than at $T^* = 0.2$. The valleys between peaks no longer reach zero cleanly; there is now a small but nonzero probability of finding an atom at interstitial distances, as occasional atoms acquire enough energy to move out of their lattice pockets and explore the space between shells.

Despite this, the overall structure is still solid. Long-range order persists: the fifth and sixth coordination shells are still discernible, and the curve has not flattened to 1.0 within the simulation box. The FCC lattice is under stress but intact.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{$T = 97.48$\,K ($T^* = 0.600$): Approaching Melting}{T = 97.5 K: Approaching Melting}}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figures/melting_gr_97k.pdf}
    \caption{g(r) of Kr at T* = 0.6 (97.48 K)}
    \label{fig:placeholder}
\end{figure}
This is the last temperature at which the $g(r)$ still looks predominantly solid-like, but signs of disorder are clear. The first peak has dropped to $g \approx 3.3$ and widened substantially. The second and third peaks, previously well-separated, have begun to merge into a single broad hump. Beyond $r \approx 8$\,\AA, the oscillations are barely above 1.0 and are essentially indistinguishable from background fluctuations.

The atoms are still mostly sitting near their lattice sites, but the thermal vibrations are now large enough that the distinction between being in a lattice pocket and between pockets is beginning to blur. The system is close to the transition.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{$T = 129.97$\,K ($T^* = 0.800$): At/Near the Melting Transition}{T = 130 K: Near the Melting Transition}}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figures/melting_gr_129k.pdf}
    \caption{g(r) of Kr at T* = 0.8 (129.97K)}
    \label{fig:placeholder}
\end{figure}
Here the character of the $g(r)$ changes qualitatively. The first peak is now broad and rounded ($g \approx 2.7$) rather than sharp and spiky. All peaks beyond the second have merged into a single featureless broad line that quickly decays to 1.0 by $r \approx 8$\,\AA. Long-range order is gone: beyond the second shell, the system looks like a liquid.

This is the hallmark of the \textbf{solid-to-liquid transition}. The FCC lattice has collapsed and atoms are no longer confined to fixed sites; instead they diffuse freely through the system. The first peak survives because liquid atoms still cannot overlap each other (the LJ repulsive core enforces a minimum approach distance), so there is always a well-defined first coordination shell even in a disordered liquid. Beyond that shell, there is no further structural information.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{$T = 162.46$\,K ($T^* = 1.000$): Liquid}{T = 162.5 K: Liquid}}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figures/melting_gr_162k.pdf}
    \caption{g(r) of Kr at T* = 1.0 (162.46 K)}
    \label{fig:placeholder}
\end{figure}
The final $g(r)$ is unambiguously liquid. There is one broad first peak centred near $r \approx 4$\,\AA, a weak second shoulder near $r \approx 7.5$\,\AA, and then a flat line at $g(r) = 1.0$. No further structure survives. The system has \emph{short-range order only}: a liquid atom knows its immediate neighbours are about 4\,\AA\ away, but has no information about where atoms beyond $\sim 8$\,\AA\ sit.

The peak value at this temperature ($g \approx 2.7$) is the same as at $T^* = 0.8$, which is consistent with both being in the liquid phase: once melting is complete, raising the temperature further primarily widens and lowers the first peak rather than dramatically changing its height.

\subsection{Why Is the System Still Liquid at $T = 162.46$\,K?}

The experimental boiling point of Krypton at atmospheric pressure is $T_\text{boil}^\text{exp} = 119.93$\,K, which is more than 40\,K below our final simulation temperature. Yet the $g(r)$ at $T^* = 1.0$ shows a clear liquid. The reason is that the simulation is run in the $NVT$ ensemble: the volume is fixed, so the density $\rho^* = 0.8442$ never changes regardless of temperature.

In a real open-atmosphere experiment, heating liquid Krypton past its boiling point allows the atoms to expand outward, dropping the density and crossing into the vapour phase. Here, the box walls prevent any such expansion. Keeping $N$ and $V$ fixed while raising $T$ is thermodynamically equivalent to applying an increasing external pressure on the system. This elevated pressure shifts the boiling point well above $T = 162.46$\,K, keeping the system on the compressed-liquid branch of the phase diagram.The simulation is not incorrect; it is simply modelling a high-pressure compressed liquid rather than Krypton at 1\,atm.

\subsection{Estimating the Melting Point}

To estimate the melting point, we track the height of the first minimum of $g(r)$ as a function of temperature. In a solid, successive coordination shells produce sharp peaks separated by deep minima (first-minimum height $\approx 0$); as the system melts, interstitial atoms fill these minima and the height rises toward unity. We search for this minimum in the range $1.2 < r^* < 1.8$ at each temperature and record its value.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figures/melting_point_analysis.pdf}
    \caption{g(rmin) vs T* plot}
    \label{fig:melt}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:melt} shows the resulting curve. At low temperatures ($T^* \lesssim 0.2$), the first minimum is essentially zero, confirming a well-ordered solid. The height then rises monotonically, with the steepest increase occurring between $T^* \approx 0.55$ and $T^* \approx 0.75$, indicating rapid structural disordering. Above $T^* \approx 0.75$ the curve flattens and saturates near $0.60$--$0.62$, characteristic of a fully liquid $g(r)$ with no residual shell structure. The melting point is identified as the midpoint of this steep rise:
\begin{equation}
    T^*_\text{melt} \approx 0.70 \quad \Rightarrow \quad
    T_\text{melt} \approx 0.70 \times 162.46\,\text{K} \approx 114\,\text{K}
    \label{eq:melt_est}
\end{equation}
This estimate agrees well with two reference values:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{LJ melting point} at $\rho^* = 0.8442$: $T^*_\text{melt} \approx 0.69$ from the established LJ phase diagram.
    \item \textbf{Experimental melting point of Krypton} at atmospheric pressure: $T_\text{melt}^\text{exp} = 115.79$\,K.
\end{itemize}
The agreement with the experimental value (roughly 1.5\,K off) is very good given the resolution of our temperature stepping. This validates the Lennard-Jones model for noble gas systems: despite being a two-parameter pairwise potential with no quantum corrections, it places the melting point of Krypton to within 2\% of experiment.


\subsection{Why Is This a First-Order Transition?}

A final point worth noting: the $g(r)$ curves do not evolve \emph{continuously} as temperature increases. Between $T = 97.48$\,K and $T = 129.97$\,K, the structural fingerprint changes \emph{qualitatively}, from multi-shell crystalline to single-peak liquid, rather than just quantitatively. This discontinuity in structural order is the signature of a \textbf{first-order phase transition}: the system does not pass through intermediate states, but undergoes an abrupt reorganisation from one phase to another. Real Krypton melts in exactly this way, with a discontinuous change in density, latent heat, and structural order at the transition. Our simulation, despite its small size ($N = 256$ atoms) and classical approximations, captures this thermodynamic behaviour.

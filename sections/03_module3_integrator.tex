\section{Module 3: The Velocity Verlet Integrator}

\subsection{What Are We Doing?}

At the heart of any MD simulation lies a really simple question: given where every atom is \emph{right now} and how fast it is moving, where will it be a moment from now? This module answers that question. We need an algorithm to evolve atomic positions and velocities forward in time by numerically solving Newton's second law, $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$, for every atom at every timestep.

We do this \emph{numerically}. Rather than calculating a single, all encompassing formula for the atoms' trajectories, we break continuous time into tiny discrete intervals $\delta t^*$ and approximate their motion step by step. The algorithm that does this for us is the \textbf{Velocity Verlet integrator}, it serves as the core of modern MD. But this computational effort gives rise to the question:

\subsection{Why Are We Doing It?}

\subsubsection{Why Can't We Solve This Analytically?}

Even for just two atoms, the LJ force depends nonlinearly on the separation $r^*$. The moment we add a third atom, every atom's force depends on two \emph{simultaneously moving} neighbours, and those neighbours each depend on everyone else. For $N$ atoms, you have $3N$ coupled, nonlinear second-order differential equations, all entangled with each other. The trajectory of atom $i$ cannot be written down without knowing the trajectory of all other atoms first. No analytical formula exists that can untangle this mess for $N > 2$. We must discretize the time and solve it step by step.

\subsubsection{Why Velocity Verlet Over Simple Euler Integration?}

The simplest possible approach, the Euler method, updates each atom using only the information available at the very start of the time step:
$$\mathbf{r}_i(t+\delta t) \approx \mathbf{r}_i(t) + \mathbf{v}_i(t)\,\delta t, \qquad \mathbf{v}_i(t+\delta t) \approx \mathbf{v}_i(t) + \mathbf{a}_i(t)\,\delta t$$
This looks perfectly logical, but it has a fatal flaw: it assumes the force acting on the atom remains completely constant throughout the entire time step $\delta t$. Imagine driving on a winding road, but you are only allowed to adjust your steering wheel once every few seconds, locking it in place in between. Because you are ignoring how the curve of the road changes during those few seconds, you will continuously drift further and further off your path. Similarly, in an Euler simulation, using a fixed and outdated force to push an atom forward causes small errors to continually grow in the same direction. Because of this the total energy of the system doesn't just fluctuate; it drifts, steadily growing or shrinking until the simulation blows up and becomes physically meaningless.

The Velocity Verlet algorithm fixes this issue by briefly looking ahead before updating the motion. Instead of assuming the force is constant, it updates the velocity using an average of the forces at \emph{both} the old and the new positions:
$$\mathbf{v}_i(t+\delta t) = \mathbf{v}_i(t) + \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[\mathbf{a}_i(t) + \mathbf{a}_i(t+\delta t)\bigr]\,\delta t$$
Coming back to our driving analogy, this is like checking the curve of the road at your destination and averaging it with your starting steering angle to make a much smoother, more accurate turn. By factoring in how the forces are changing \emph{during} the movement, the overshoots and undershoots naturally cancel each other out. Because of this balanced approach, the numerical errors don't grow. The system's total energy stays stable, oscillating within a very tight band around its true conserved value forever, which is exactly what we require.

\subsection{How Is It Implemented?}

\subsubsection{The Four-Step Algorithm}

Each timestep $\delta t^*$ proceeds through four operations. In reduced units, $m^* = 1$, so acceleration equals force directly.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Update positions} using the current velocity and acceleration:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbf{r}_i(t+\delta t) = \mathbf{r}_i(t) + \mathbf{v}_i(t)\,\delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}_i(t)\,\delta t^2
        \label{eq:vv_pos}
    \end{equation}
    PBC wrapping (Module 2) is applied immediately after to keep all atoms inside the box.

    \item \textbf{Half-step velocity}; advance velocities halfway through the interval using the \emph{old} forces:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbf{v}_i\!\left(t + \tfrac{1}{2}\delta t\right) = \mathbf{v}_i(t) + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}_i(t)\,\delta t
        \label{eq:vv_vhalf}
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Recompute forces} from the new positions. This is the most computationally intensive step, and it must happen \emph{after} moving the atoms and \emph{before} completing the velocities.

    \item \textbf{Complete velocity update} using the \emph{new} forces:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbf{v}_i(t + \delta t) = \mathbf{v}_i\!\left(t + \tfrac{1}{2}\delta t\right) + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}_i(t+\delta t)\,\delta t
        \label{eq:vv_vfull}
    \end{equation}
    Steps 2 and 4 together implement the force averaging that gives Velocity Verlet its stability.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Kinetic and Potential Energy}

In reduced units ($m^* = 1$), the kinetic and total energies are:
\begin{align}
    K^* &= \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{N} |\mathbf{v}_i^*|^2 \label{eq:ke}\\
    E^* &= K^* + U^* \label{eq:etot}
\end{align}
where $U^*$ is the sum of shifted LJ pair potentials computed in Module 1.

\subsubsection{Choosing the Time Step and the $10^{-4}$ Rule}

The reduced time unit is $t^* = t/t_0$, where $t_0 = \sigma\sqrt{m/\varepsilon}$. For Krypton, $t_0 \approx 2.16$\,ps. The time step must be small enough to resolve the fastest atomic vibrations, roughly 50 steps per vibrational period, while being large enough for the simulation to cover meaningful physical time without excessive computation.

The accepted stability criterion is:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\Delta E^*}{\langle E^* \rangle} \lesssim 10^{-4}
    \label{eq:fluct_criterion}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta E^*$ is the root-mean-square fluctuation of total energy in the production run. If the fluctuation exceeds this threshold, the time step must be reduced. We use $\delta t^* = 0.005$ for the module test and for the full engine runs.

\subsection{Test Results}

To verify the integrator, we ran a 500-step simulation with $N = 108$ atoms ($n_\text{cells} = 3$) on an FCC lattice at $\rho^* = 0.8$ and $T^* \approx 1.0$, with $\delta t^* = 0.005$ and $r_c^* = 2.5$.

    \label{fig:m3_energy}

The oscillations of $K^*$ and $U^*$ seen in Figure~\ref{fig:m3_energy} are a direct reflection of the system's physical dynamics. The simulation begins with atoms arranged in a tight, artificial grid. As the system naturally expands and reaches a more stable state, the atoms rapidly accelerate, converting built up potential energy into kinetic energy. They continue to continuously trade energy back and forth as they interact.

The most important part of this graph is the red line. Despite the immense internal shifts between kinetic and potential energy, the Total Energy ($E^*$) remains flat. This constant $E^*$ proves that our Velocity Verlet integrator is stable and conservative; it successfully tracks the complex atomic movements without unintentionally losing or generating energy due to integration errors.

\subsubsection{What Happens When the Time Step Is Too Large?}

Figure~\ref{fig:m3_dt_compare} illustrates the consequence of choosing an overly large $\delta t^*$. With $\delta t^* = 0.005$ (left panel), the total energy of a two-atom test system oscillates tightly around a constant mean; the integrator is stable. With $\delta t^* = 0.05$ (right panel), the energy immediately begins to climb and diverges rapidly within a few dozen steps, reaching unphysical scales in the order of $10^{33}$. This happens because the core assumption, that the LJ interaction remains approximately constant across one time interval, breaks down completely when $\delta t^*$ is too large. The atoms drastically overshoot their equilibrium positions, generating huge repulsive forces that introduce unrealistic energy into the system.

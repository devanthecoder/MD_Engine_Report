\section{Module 3: The Velocity Verlet Integrator}

\subsection{What Are We Doing?}

At the heart of any MD simulation lies a deceptively simple question: given where every atom is \emph{right now} and how fast it is moving, where will it be a moment from now? This module answers that question. We need an algorithm to evolve atomic positions and velocities forward in time by numerically solving Newton's second law, $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$, for every atom at every timestep.

The key word is \emph{numerically}. Because the force on each atom depends on the positions of all its neighbours — and those positions are themselves changing — there is no closed-form analytical solution. Instead, we break continuous time into tiny discrete intervals $\delta t^*$ and approximate the trajectory step by step. The algorithm that does this for us is the \textbf{Velocity Verlet integrator}, the workhorse of modern MD.

\subsection{Why Are We Doing It?}

\subsubsection{Why Can't We Solve This Analytically?}

Even for just two atoms, the LJ force depends nonlinearly on the separation $r^*$. The moment you add a third atom, every atom's force depends on two \emph{simultaneously moving} neighbours — and those neighbours each depend on everyone else. For $N$ atoms, you have $3N$ coupled, nonlinear second-order differential equations, all entangled with each other. The trajectory of atom $i$ cannot be written down without knowing the trajectory of all other atoms first. No analytical formula exists that can untangle this mess for $N > 2$. We must discretise time and solve it step by step.

\subsubsection{Why Velocity Verlet Over Simple Euler Integration?}

The simplest possible approach — the Euler method — moves each atom using only the force at the current position:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{r}_i(t+\delta t) \approx \mathbf{r}_i(t) + \mathbf{v}_i(t)\,\delta t, \qquad
    \mathbf{v}_i(t+\delta t) \approx \mathbf{v}_i(t) + \mathbf{a}_i(t)\,\delta t
\end{equation}
This looks reasonable but hides a fatal flaw. The force at the \emph{old} position is used to predict the \emph{new} position, but once the atom has moved, the actual force at the new position is different. This inconsistency — using yesterday's force to make today's decision — means numerical errors build up monotonically at every step. The total energy of an Euler-integrated system does not oscillate around the correct value; it \emph{drifts}, systematically climbing or falling until the simulation becomes unphysical.

The Velocity Verlet algorithm fixes this by using the forces at \emph{both} the old and new positions to update the velocity:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{v}_i(t+\delta t) = \mathbf{v}_i(t) + \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[\mathbf{a}_i(t) + \mathbf{a}_i(t+\delta t)\bigr]\,\delta t
\end{equation}
This averaging makes the algorithm \textbf{time-reversible} (running it backwards recovers the original trajectory) and \textbf{symplectic} (it conserves the geometric structure of phase space). The practical consequence: the total energy does not drift. Instead, it oscillates within a narrow band around the true conserved value indefinitely. This is exactly what a physically correct NVE simulation demands.

\subsection{How Is It Implemented?}

\subsubsection{The Four-Step Algorithm}

Each timestep $\delta t^*$ proceeds through four operations. In reduced units, $m^* = 1$, so acceleration equals force directly.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Update positions} using the current velocity and acceleration:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbf{r}_i(t+\delta t) = \mathbf{r}_i(t) + \mathbf{v}_i(t)\,\delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}_i(t)\,\delta t^2
        \label{eq:vv_pos}
    \end{equation}
    PBC wrapping (Module 2) is applied immediately after to keep all atoms inside the box.

    \item \textbf{Half-step velocity} — advance velocities halfway through the interval using the \emph{old} forces:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbf{v}_i\!\left(t + \tfrac{1}{2}\delta t\right) = \mathbf{v}_i(t) + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}_i(t)\,\delta t
        \label{eq:vv_vhalf}
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Recompute forces} from the new positions (Module 1 + 2 logic). This is the most expensive step, and it must happen \emph{after} moving the atoms and \emph{before} completing the velocities.

    \item \textbf{Complete velocity update} using the \emph{new} forces:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbf{v}_i(t + \delta t) = \mathbf{v}_i\!\left(t + \tfrac{1}{2}\delta t\right) + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}_i(t+\delta t)\,\delta t
        \label{eq:vv_vfull}
    \end{equation}
    Steps 2 and 4 together implement the force-averaging that gives Velocity Verlet its superior stability.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Kinetic and Potential Energy}

In reduced units ($m^* = 1$), the kinetic and total energies are:
\begin{align}
    K^* &= \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{N} |\mathbf{v}_i^*|^2 \label{eq:ke}\\
    E^* &= K^* + U^* \label{eq:etot}
\end{align}
where $U^*$ is the sum of shifted LJ pair potentials computed in Module 1.

\subsubsection{Choosing the Time Step and the $10^{-4}$ Rule}

The reduced time unit is $t^* = t/t_0$, where $t_0 = \sigma\sqrt{m/\varepsilon}$. For Krypton, $t_0 \approx 2.16$\,ps. The time step must be small enough to resolve the fastest atomic vibrations — roughly 50 steps per vibrational period — while being large enough for the simulation to cover meaningful physical time without excessive computation.

The accepted stability criterion is:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\Delta E^*}{\langle E^* \rangle} \lesssim 10^{-4}
    \label{eq:fluct_criterion}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta E^*$ is the root-mean-square fluctuation of total energy in the production run. If the fluctuation exceeds this threshold, the time step must be reduced. We use $\delta t^* = 0.001$ for the module test and $\delta t^* = 0.005$ for the full engine runs.

\subsection{Test Results}

To verify the integrator, we ran a 200-step simulation with $N = 108$ atoms ($n_\text{cells} = 3$) on an FCC lattice at $\rho^* = 0.8$ and $T^* \approx 1.0$, with $\delta t^* = 0.001$ and $r_c^* = 2.5$.

The atoms start on a rigid lattice and immediately begin to relax; potential energy converts to kinetic and back. The crucial test is whether these fluctuations cancel. \subsubsection{What Happens When the Time Step Is Too Large?}

Figure~\ref{fig:m3_dt_compare} illustrates the consequence of choosing an overly large $\delta t^*$. With $\delta t^* = 0.005$ (left panel), the total energy of a two-atom test system oscillates tightly around a constant mean — the integrator is stable. With $\delta t^* = 0.05$ (right panel), the energy immediately begins to climb and diverges catastrophically within a few dozen steps. This happens because the force assumption — that the LJ interaction remains approximately constant across one time interval — breaks down completely when $\delta t^*$ is too large; atoms overshoot their equilibrium positions, generating enormous repulsive forces that inject energy spuriously into the system.
